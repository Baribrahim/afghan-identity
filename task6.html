Examine Traditions and Celebrations Abroad

Afghan Traditions We Celebrate

  • Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha: Religious holidays with family gatherings
  • Family Gatherings: Regular extended family meetings
  • Wedding Traditions: Maintaining Afghan wedding customs when attending ceremonies

How Traditions Have Changed

Eid Celebrations: Without a large Afghan community nearby, our Eid celebrations are smaller and more family-centered. However, we connected with other Afghan families in Qatar. Now we have a small community that celebrates together.

Language: Some traditions that were once explained in Dari are now sometimes explained in a mix of languages. This is especially true for younger family members who are more comfortable in English.

Easier to Maintain

  • Family meals and cooking traditions (can be done anywhere)
  • Religious practices and prayers
  • Language use at home
  • Storytelling and oral history
  • Values and behavioral norms

Harder to Maintain

  • Large community celebrations
  • Connection to extended family in Afghanistan
  • Traditional clothing in daily life
  • Participation in Afghan cultural events
  • Understanding of regional variations in traditions

Impact on Sense of Belonging

Celebrating traditions abroad creates a mixed feeling of belonging. On one hand, keeping these traditions makes me feel connected to my Afghan heritage and family. On the other hand, the changes and smaller scale sometimes make me feel like I am experiencing a weaker version of the culture.

However, I learned that changing traditions does not mean losing them. It means the culture is alive and growing. My family's way of celebrating cultural holidays in Qatar is still truly Afghan, just changed to fit our situation. This taught me that cultural identity is not about copying traditions perfectly. It is about keeping their meaning and importance while changing them for new situations.

The challenge is balancing between keeping things authentic and changing them for practical reasons. This struggle has become part of my identity. I am Afghan, but I am also someone who learned to keep that identity across different countries and situations.
